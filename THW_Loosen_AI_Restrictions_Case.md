THW LOOSEN RESTRICTIONS ON AI COMPANIES
Full Case Package | PM-Ready | March 27, 2026


BACKGROUND (92 words)

AI regulation is at an inflection point. The EU AI Act — the world's most comprehensive AI law — entered force August 1, 2024 with phased compliance deadlines, but on March 19, 2026, Parliament committees voted 101-9 to postpone key high-risk obligations to December 2, 2027 (standalone) and August 2, 2028 (product-embedded), because standards and guidance simply weren't ready. In the United States, there is no comprehensive federal AI statute; instead, a patchwork of state laws has triggered a December 11, 2025 executive order pushing for a "minimally burdensome national standard." Both developments illustrate governments themselves recognizing that current regulatory architecture is mismatched to AI's pace.

Definition: "Loosen restrictions" means reducing regulatory barriers to AI development and deployment while retaining baseline consumer protection, antitrust enforcement, and criminal law. We do NOT propose deregulation — we propose smarter, more targeted regulation.

Caveat: Gov does not need to defend removing ALL regulations. We defend a shift from precautionary, pre-market compliance regimes toward iterative, deployment-stage governance with targeted prohibitions on concrete harms.


FRAMEWORK: Inevitability + Jurisdictional Choice

1. First on framing: the question is not WHETHER AI develops, but WHERE and UNDER WHAT CONDITIONS. Three warrants:

   a. Inevitability of capability diffusion — AI capabilities spread globally regardless of any single jurisdiction's rules. Open-source frontier models are published openly; compute access is increasingly distributed; talent is globally mobile. No regulatory regime can prevent AI from existing — it can only determine where the accountable development happens.
      i. The Stanford HAI AI Index 2025 reports $109.1 billion in U.S. private AI investment in 2024 — twelve times China's $9.3 billion. But China leads the world in AI publications and patents, and the quality gap between U.S. and Chinese models narrowed to near-parity in 2024. Capability is diffusing regardless of who invests more.
      ii. Open-source releases (Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek, Qwen) mean that once a capability exists anywhere, it exists everywhere within months. Regulation cannot un-invent a technique.

   b. Jurisdictional choice determines accountability, not existence — If democratic nations impose friction that pushes development into less transparent ecosystems, the result is not "safer AI" but "less accountable AI." The question for the judge is: do you want AI developed under institutions with press freedom, judicial review, and enforceable consumer law — or developed wherever compliance costs are lowest?
      i. The European Parliament itself acknowledges this dynamic: its own studies note EU startups and scaleups frequently relocate to the U.S. for venture capital and compute access, driven in part by regulatory burden.
      ii. The January 23, 2025 U.S. executive order explicitly frames this as a competition: directing agencies to "sustain and enhance America's global AI dominance" and to "suspend, revise, or rescind" actions that create "barriers" to U.S. leadership.

   c. Evaluate by comparative governance, not utopia — The judge should ask: "Which regulatory posture produces the least-bad world?" — not "Is AI risky?" We agree AI is risky. The disagreement is whether broad pre-market compliance regimes or targeted post-deployment enforcement better manages those risks.
      i. If Opp's framework is "any loosening creates danger," they must defend the status quo's own costs: delayed medical diagnostics, regulatory arbitrage, compliance regimes that protect incumbents and crush startups.
      ii. We're asking the judge to weigh demonstrated costs of over-regulation against speculative benefits of precautionary friction.


ARGUMENT 1: Current Regulatory Architecture Imposes Concrete, Measurable Costs

2. Our first independent argument is that the compliance substrate of current AI regulation creates measurable economic drag that disproportionately harms smaller firms and delays deployment. Three sub-claims:

   a. The compliance tax is real and regressive — Regulatory complexity functions as a fixed cost that large firms absorb but small firms cannot:
      i. EIB Survey — A European Parliament study citing EIB data found that a meaningful share of EU startups dedicate a significant portion of staff to regulatory tasks. When you have 12 employees and 2 of them are doing compliance paperwork, that's a competitive death sentence against Google, which has a 500-person policy team.
      ii. GDPR as precedent — GDPR rollout led to a 26% reduction in EU venture capital investment in technology relative to the United States, a 21% reduction in the number of EU deals led by U.S. investors, and an estimated $1.6 billion per year reduction in transatlantic investment flows. New ventures seeking first-time funding were hit hardest — meaning the pipeline of future competitors to Big Tech was choked.
      iii. Substitution Effect — When compliance costs rise, capital doesn't disappear — it migrates. The firms that leave aren't the dangerous ones; they're the innovative ones that can't afford legal departments. The firms that stay are the incumbents who can treat compliance as a moat.

   b. Regulatory uncertainty delays deployment of beneficial systems — The gap between a regulation being announced and its implementation details becoming clear is a dead zone where no rational firm invests:
      i. The EU's own admission — The EU Parliament voted to postpone high-risk AI obligations from August 2026 to December 2, 2027 and August 2, 2028 specifically because standards weren't ready. This isn't us speculating that uncertainty is harmful — this is the regulators themselves saying "we set deadlines before we knew what compliance would look like, and companies can't plan around rules that don't exist yet."
      ii. Real product delays — Meta explicitly paused or limited EU AI feature rollouts in 2024, citing regulatory unpredictability. These aren't dangerous products — they're consumer features that millions of people in other markets already use.
      iii. Medical AI opportunity costs — In breast cancer screening, AI-assisted workflows have demonstrated the ability to raise detection rates and reduce clinician workload. The FDA has responded intelligently with guidance enabling iterative improvement through "predetermined change control plans." But static pre-market approval regimes in other jurisdictions mean these gains arrive years later for patients outside the U.S. — and those years have a body count.

   c. Fragmentation is itself a harm — When rules differ across jurisdictions without coordination, compliance becomes combinatorially complex:
      i. The U.S. patchwork — The December 11, 2025 executive order explicitly frames the problem: "a patchwork of state laws could lead to burdensome compliance regimes and harm innovation necessary for global AI leadership." The order created an AI Litigation Task Force to challenge certain state laws. When even pro-regulation governments are loosening to reduce fragmentation, that IS the argument for our motion.
      ii. Cross-border compliance multiplication — A startup building an AI hiring tool must simultaneously comply with the EU AI Act's high-risk rules, Colorado's algorithmic discrimination ban, NYC's Local Law 144, and potentially dozens of other overlapping regimes — each with different definitions, audit requirements, and liability standards.


ARGUMENT 2: Loosening Enables Better Governance, Not Less Governance

3. Our second independent argument is that targeted, iterative regulation is categorically more effective than broad precautionary compliance at managing AI risks. Three sub-claims:

   a. Governments are already moving toward our model — We're not proposing something radical; we're describing what's already happening:
      i. The EU's own reform — The Digital Omnibus simplification process simultaneously postpones broad high-risk compliance AND adds a specific ban on "nudifier" systems (AI that creates non-consensual explicit images) — passed 569 to 45. This is our model in action: remove premature blanket obligations while imposing surgical prohibitions on concrete harms.
      ii. FDA iterative approval — The U.S. FDA's guidance on AI-enabled medical devices allows predetermined change control plans — meaning the device can update and improve post-deployment within pre-approved parameters. This is "regulated iteration," not deregulation.
      iii. The pattern — In every jurisdiction, the trajectory is the same: ambitious broad regulation → implementation problems → targeted adjustments. We're asking the judge to arrive at the destination faster.

   b. Precautionary regulation creates false security while missing real threats:
      i. Documentation vs. detection — The AI Act's high-risk requirements emphasize transparency, documentation, and dataset governance. These are important — but they don't prevent a determined bad actor from deploying harmful systems. They create a paper trail that law-abiding firms complete and bad actors ignore.
      ii. Fairness metrics are incoherent without specification — Kleinberg, Chouldechova, and others prove that common statistical fairness criteria are mathematically incompatible: you cannot simultaneously satisfy calibration, false positive rate parity, and false negative rate parity except in trivial cases. Regulators who require "bias mitigation" without specifying WHICH fairness notion create compliance that is literally impossible to satisfy.
      iii. Targeted prohibitions work better — The nudifier ban shows how to regulate AI effectively: identify a specific, concrete harm with a clear causal mechanism, and ban it. Enforceable, clear, no collateral drag.

   c. Speed of deployment is itself a safety mechanism:
      i. Data-enabled learning — AI advantage compounds because learning from deployment data compounds. Systems that are deployed, monitored, and updated iteratively become safer faster than systems locked behind years of pre-market approval.
      ii. The counterfactual of delay — If a cancer-screening AI that could have caught 15% more tumors is delayed by 2 years of regulatory process, the patients who die in those 2 years are real. The risks of deployment are detectable and correctable. The risks of non-deployment are invisible and permanent.
      iii. Democratic AI advantage — If democratic nations develop and deploy AI faster, they set global norms and safety practices. If they slow down, authoritarian states set those norms. The Stanford data shows the quality gap is closing — the window is narrow and shrinking.


STRATEGIC CONCESSION MAP

What we concede (and why it doesn't matter under our framework):

"AI can cause real harms" — Conceded. We never said otherwise. The question is which governance model minimizes those harms.

"Some regulation is necessary" — Conceded. We're not libertarians. We support consumer protection, criminal law enforcement, and targeted prohibitions. The motion says "loosen," not "abolish."

"Corporations have bad incentives sometimes" — Conceded. That's why we retain enforcement mechanisms. But bad incentives are an argument for oversight, not for compliance-heavy pre-market regimes that protect incumbents.

What we do NOT concede:
- That broad precautionary regulation produces net safety benefits
- That the status quo's costs are acceptable
- That slowing democratic AI development makes the world safer


A2 "AI poses existential/catastrophic risk"

1. Concession: We take catastrophic risk seriously. A world where advanced AI systems cause large-scale harm is genuinely terrifying.

2. Turn — their model makes catastrophic risk worse:
   a. Who do you want at the frontier? If democratic nations slow down, the frontier moves to actors with less transparency, less accountability, and less institutional capacity for safety research. If you believe frontier AI is dangerous, you should want it developed in jurisdictions with free press, academic critique, and judicial review.
   b. Safety research requires capability research. You cannot build effective safety tools for systems you haven't built. Alignment research, interpretability tools, and red-teaming all require access to frontier models.
   c. The EU AI Act's GPAI provisions create obligations for "systemic risk" models, but the compliance architecture depends on Codes of Practice still being developed. Even the most comprehensive regime doesn't have functioning frontier enforcement yet.

3. Alternative: Targeted international governance focused on compute governance, safety evaluations, and deployment monitoring — not blanket compliance regimes.


A2 "Deepfakes and surveillance"

1. Concession: Generative AI misuse is real and already causing harm. Non-consensual intimate imagery, disinformation, and voice cloning fraud are genuine problems.

2. Turn — broad regulation doesn't prevent misuse; targeted prohibition does:
   a. Bad actors already exploit generative tools regardless of compliance obligations on legitimate developers. The question is whether you slow down the legitimate actors who could build detection and provenance tools.
   b. The EU's own reform illustrates the right approach: delay broad compliance while passing a specific nudifier ban (569-45 vote). Surgical, enforceable, no collateral drag.
   c. Synthetic media detection requires rapid iteration — exactly what precautionary regulation slows.

3. Alternative: Criminal law for misuse + technical countermeasures (watermarking, provenance, detection) + specific bans on defined harmful applications.


A2 "Bias and fairness concerns"

1. Concession: AI systems can and do perpetuate or amplify biases. Serious problem, serious governance needed.

2. Turn — current regulation makes the bias problem LESS solvable:
   a. Fairness impossibility — Kleinberg et al. and Chouldechova prove that common fairness criteria are mutually incompatible. Regulators who mandate "bias mitigation" without specifying which metric create mathematically incoherent compliance.
   b. The AI Act's requirements depend on standards that don't exist yet — which is why the EU is delaying implementation. You cannot comply with requirements that haven't been specified.
   c. The December 2025 U.S. EO criticizes Colorado's discrimination ban for potentially "forcing AI models to produce false results" — illustrating how vague mandates make systems LESS accurate.

3. Alternative: Require algorithmic auditing with specified fairness metrics (domain-specific), mandate transparency about tradeoffs, enforce through post-deployment monitoring.


A2 "Corporations can't be trusted"

1. Concession: Corporate incentives don't naturally align with public safety.

2. Turn — regulatory capture makes this worse under their model:
   a. Complex compliance regimes disproportionately benefit large corporations who can afford legal teams. Google treats AI Act compliance as a competitive moat. Startups cannot.
   b. GDPR didn't make Big Tech more trustworthy — it killed smaller competitors. EU VC investment fell 26% relative to the U.S. The firms that disappeared weren't the dangerous ones.
   c. When regulatory complexity increases, the return on lobbying increases. Incumbent firms shape rules to protect their position. Precautionary regulation makes rent-seeking more profitable.

3. Alternative: Strong antitrust + consumer protection + liability for demonstrable harms + mandatory incident reporting. Accountable for outcomes, not paperwork.
