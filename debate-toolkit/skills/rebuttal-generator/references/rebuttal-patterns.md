# Common Rebuttal Patterns in Parliamentary Debate

## Pattern Library

Quick-reference patterns for dismantling common argument types.

### Against "Ban X" arguments
- Who enforces the ban? What are the enforcement costs?
- Does banning push the activity underground where it's harder to regulate?
- Are there less restrictive alternatives that achieve the same goal?
- Historical precedent: Prohibition, War on Drugs, censorship backfires

### Against "Economic harm" arguments
- Short-term cost vs. long-term benefit: transitions always have friction
- Whose economic harm? Concentrated losses vs. distributed gains
- Counter with GDP data, employment figures, or trade statistics from comparable policies
- Is the economic model they're using realistic? (Static vs. dynamic analysis)

### Against "Slippery slope" arguments
- What is the mechanism that makes the slope slippery?
- Identify real-world cases where the slope didn't happen
- Point out that legal and institutional safeguards exist
- "By that logic, we could never change anything"

### Against "Rights-based" arguments
- Rights conflict: whose rights are being weighed against whose?
- Distinguish negative rights (freedom from) vs. positive rights (entitlement to)
- Even fundamental rights have limits (classic: "fire in a crowded theater")
- Proportionality: does the rights infringement match the harm being prevented?

### Against "Utilitarian / greatest good" arguments
- Who defines "the greatest good"? Challenge the measurement
- Tyranny of the majority: some outcomes are wrong regardless of aggregate benefit
- Are they counting all the affected parties, or ignoring marginalized groups?
- Short-term utility vs. long-term systemic effects

### Against "Status quo is fine" arguments
- Identify who the status quo harms (often invisible or marginalized groups)
- Show that the problem is getting worse over time (trend data)
- The status quo is not neutral; it reflects past choices that can be re-evaluated
- Opportunity cost: what are we losing by not acting?

### Against "International examples" arguments
- Context matters: different political systems, cultures, economies
- Cherry-picking: are they showing the success cases and ignoring the failures?
- Provide counterexamples from countries with different outcomes
- Is the comparison actually analogous, or are there confounding variables?

## Rhetorical Techniques for Delivery

- **The reframe**: "They call it freedom; we call it abandonment."
- **The concede-and-pivot**: "Even if we grant their point on X, it doesn't change Y, which is what matters."
- **The weight shift**: "The question isn't whether some people benefit -- it's whether the harm to others outweighs that."
- **The absurdity test**: "If their logic were correct, we'd also have to accept Z, which no one believes."
- **The missing actor**: "They describe the problem but never explain who solves it, how, or at what cost."
